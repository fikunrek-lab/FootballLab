Tactical Think-Piece

Is Football Becoming Too Tactical?

Football today is smarter, faster, and more structured than ever. But has the modern game become too controlled?

Football Is More Organized Than Ever

Football today is smarter, faster, and more structured than ever. But sometimes, it also feels less spontaneous. This made me ask: has the modern game become too tactical?

Nowadays, managers seem to prioritize the off-ball elements of the game. Possession structure, pressing shape, build-up patterns, and positional discipline are fundamental to almost every modern manager’s system.

Modern managers cannot afford to plan only for when their best players get the ball. They also have to ask, “What happens if he loses the ball?”

Long Shots and the Obsession With Control

When taking a shot, the modern player is not only thinking about scoring. He is also thinking about the team’s structure if the shot gets blocked.

Long shots used to feel like a normal part of football, even though they were usually low-percentage chances. Today, managers often prefer working the ball into better zones to increase the xG of each shot.

xG stands for Expected Goals. It is a statistical metric in football that measures the probability that a specific shot will result in a goal.

Long shots are becoming less common because many managers would rather avoid the risk of being caught open on a counterattack than chase the reward of scoring from distance. A blocked shot can start a counterattack, and the attacking team may lose its shape.

What Football Gains

Modern tactics have improved football, too. Teams are better coached, more organized in possession, and more intelligent without the ball.

This has helped close the gap between top teams and average teams. Even teams with less individual quality can be difficult to break down because they understand pressing triggers, compact defensive shapes, and how to create high-quality chances.

What Football Loses

However, the danger is not in tactics themselves. The danger is when tactics leave no room for flair.

Football may be losing some of its individual risk-taking. The fall of Joga Bonito represents this shift. The game may be producing fewer mavericks and unpredictable dribblers in exchange for players who are trained to choose the safer option.

The Arsenal Angle

In Arsenal’s case, structure gives them control, but individual bravery gives them separation.

Arsenal under Mikel Arteta is extremely structured, but the best version of Arsenal still needs players who can break the system at the right time. They need players who can produce a moment of magic to open up a game.

Think about Bukayo Saka taking on his fullback 1v1, Eberechi Eze receiving the ball in the pockets, or Declan Rice carrying the ball through midfield. Arteta’s job is to platform his players and get them into favorable positions, but ultimately, it is the individual brilliance of the player that turns those moments into something dangerous.

Conclusion

Football is not becoming too tactical. However, you could argue that it is becoming too controlled.

As football evolves, the best teams will not be the ones that abandon structure, but the ones that know when to break it.

Football needs tactics because, without structure, the game becomes chaos. On the other hand, without risk, football loses its magic. The long shot, Joga Bonito, and the risky pass are sometimes the moments that separate good teams from great ones.
