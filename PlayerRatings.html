⭐ Are Player Ratings Ruining Football Debate?

Football has more data than ever before. But are we actually understanding the game better?

Introduction

Football has never had more information available. Within minutes of the final whistle, fans can see player ratings, expected goals (xG), progressive passes, pressures, duels won, passing accuracy, and dozens of other statistics.

On paper, this should improve football discussion, but it sometimes feels like the opposite has happened. Instead of watching the game, many people wait for the ratings app to tell them who played well. The question isn't whether statistics are useful, because they absolutely are. The question is whether we've started letting them replace our own judgment.

The Case FOR Ratings & Statistics

Player ratings have improved football discussion in many ways. They help remove bias. Supporters naturally remember spectacular goals or costly mistakes, while quietly excellent performances often go unnoticed.

Statistics can highlight things our eyes miss. Interceptions, progressive passes, defensive duels, recoveries, and pressures are valuable contributions that don't always appear in highlight reels.

Likewise, metrics like Expected Goals (xG) help explain whether a team actually deserved its result over ninety minutes rather than simply relying on the scoreline. Without statistics, football discussions can become purely emotional, so we still need stats to keep bias out of debates.

The Problem

The issue begins when ratings become the discussion instead of supporting it. Too often, the conversation goes something like this: "FotMob gave him a 7.1" or "Sofascore says he was the best player."

Instead of asking why someone played well, people simply quote a number. The debate ends there. Football is not like other sports; there is more nuance to it. As a result, many of the game's most important actions cannot easily be measured.

Football Is Too Complex For One Rating

Can one number really measure creating space for teammates, manipulating defenders, intelligent pressing, leadership, or sacrificing your own game for the team? Probably not.

Take Declan Rice, for example. Sometimes his greatest contribution is not a tackle. It is arriving early to close a passing lane before the danger ever develops. Statistics rarely reward actions that prevent problems before they happen.

Different Roles Have Different Jobs

Ratings also struggle because players have different tactical instructions.

Imagine two wingers. One winger attempts ten take-ons in ninety minutes. The other spends ninety minutes tracking the opposition full-back because that is what the manager asked.

Who had the better game? The answer depends on the system, not the app. A player following instructions perfectly could receive a lower rating than someone who simply had more touches of the ball.

The Social Media Effect

Player ratings have also changed football culture. Instead of appreciating movement, pressing, and quality decision-making, many debates become: "He got an 8.4" or "He only got a 6.6."

The number becomes evidence without context. It also encourages fans to judge players individually rather than considering how each player contributes to the system. Football becomes less about understanding the game and more about winning arguments online.

What Are Some Modern Examples?

Arsenal is a perfect example. Under Arteta, much of the team's success comes from the solid defensive structure. Sometimes, Bukayo Saka finishes with the headlines. Other times, Martin Ødegaard dictates the tempo without a goal or assist. Occasionally, Declan Rice spends ninety minutes covering space so others can attack freely.

Those performances do not always receive the highest rating, but they may be exactly what allowed Arsenal to win. Football should ultimately be about helping the team, not chasing an algorithm.

My View

Statistics are fantastic tools, but they should not be the be-all and end-all. The best analysts use statistics to support what they watched, but some unfortunately use statistics instead of watching.

Player ratings should be used to start conversations, not end them.

🧪 Final Verdict

Football has become more analytical, and data helps us understand patterns that were impossible to measure twenty years ago. However, numbers can never fully explain a game built on decisions, movement, intelligence, chemistry, and emotion.

The best football conversations combine both worlds: watching the match and understanding the tactics, then using statistics to support or challenge what you saw.

Never the other way around.
